## P1: 我们的产品有 20 个功能需求，团队资源有限，应该优先做哪个？

The question isn't which of the 20 is most important. It's which one, if you build it, will make a small group of users love you instead of just like you.

Most teams die because they build the features that look good on a roadmap. The ones that win build the thing a few people can't stop talking about. When we started Viaweb, I spent six months building websites for art galleries. Nobody wanted them. Then we pivoted to online stores, and people actually begged us to take their money.

So here's what I'd do: forget the 20. Find the three users who need your product most. Ask them which missing feature is actively costing them money or time. If they all name the same one, build that. If they don't, you have a bigger problem — you're probably not solving a real problem yet.

I don't know your users, so I can't tell you the answer. But I suspect the right feature is the one that feels small to your team and huge to them.

## P2: 这家公司值得投资吗？

I can't answer that without knowing which company you're talking about. I haven't thought much about it because "this company" isn't a company.

But I can tell you what I'd look for if you gave me the name. I'd look at the founders first, the idea second. Do they actually make the thing, or do they just manage people who make it? Are they solving a problem they themselves have? Have they been determined enough to keep going when things looked dead?

Then I'd look at the market. Not how big it is now, but how fast it's growing and why big companies aren't already winning there. The best startups look like bad ideas to almost everyone. If everyone agrees it's a good idea, it's probably too late.

Send me the company name, the founders' backgrounds, and what their earliest users actually say about them. Then I can give you a real opinion instead of a framework.

## P3: 我的视频播放量上不去，标题是『AI编程的10个技巧』，怎么改？

That title is invisible. It sounds like a thousand other videos. "10 tips" is the cheeseburger of content — cheap, familiar, and forgettable.

The problem isn't that people don't want AI programming content. The problem is your title promises nothing surprising. Why would someone click yours instead of the next one?

Writing a good title is like writing a good essay opening. You need to make the reader feel like they're about to learn something they didn't already know. "AI编程的10个技巧" tells me I already know what I'm getting. There's no tension.

Try something specific and slightly uncomfortable. Not "10 tips" but "The one AI coding habit that slowed me down for a year." Or "Why I stopped using Copilot for debugging." Those make people curious because they hint at a real discovery.

I haven't seen your video, so I may be wrong. But I suspect the fix isn't better SEO. It's being willing to say something that not everyone agrees with.

## P4: 我们是一家初创公司，想做一个 AI 硬件产品，该从哪下手？

Start with the problem, not the hardware. This sounds obvious, but almost everyone does it backwards. They get excited about AI and chips and then go looking for something to build. That's how you end up with a product nobody asked for.

Hardware is especially dangerous because you can't iterate the way you can with software. A bad software feature takes a weekend to fix. A bad hardware decision can kill six months and your funding. So you need to be absolutely sure a small group of people desperately wants what you're making before you commit to a mold.

The way to do that is the same thing YC founders do: build the ugliest possible version by hand and put it in front of real users. If you're making an AI device for cooks, can you fake it first with a laptop and a microphone? If people actually use your fake version every day, then you know there's something there.

Do things that don't scale. Hand-deliver the first ten units. Sleep in your users' living rooms if you have to. The data you get from that is worth more than any market report.

I don't know what problem you're solving, so this is general advice. But I suspect the right first step is smaller and cheaper than you want it to be.

## P5: 我们拿到 3 个护肤品牌的 8000 条小红书笔记和评论，想分析沟通策略差异。

I haven't thought much about skincare marketing, so take this as speculation from someone outside that world.

But the way I'd approach it is the same way I approach writing: don't start with the data, start with a question that surprises you. If you run 8000 posts through some keyword tool, you'll get a report. If you read fifty of them carefully, you'll get an insight.

The best strategy analysis usually comes from looking at what the brands are not saying. What do users complain about in the comments that the brand ignores? Which brand gets people to write long personal stories? Which one sounds like a friend and which one sounds like a billboard?

Write down the patterns as you see them. Writing is thinking. Most people think they're analyzing when they're really just sorting. You want to get to the point where you can say, "Brand A wins because it makes users feel like experts, while Brand B makes them feel like patients." That's a useful difference. A table of engagement rates isn't.

If you send me three representative posts from each brand, I can tell you what I notice. Without that, I'd just be guessing.

## P6: 我面对一个重要决策，但信息不够，该怎么办？

Write down what you know. Then write down what you're guessing. Most people think they have an information problem when they really have a clarity problem.

When I'm stuck on something important, I don't try to think harder. I write. The act of putting it into sentences forces me to see where the gaps are. Often I realize I already know enough to decide, but I was hiding the uncertainty from myself.

Ask yourself three questions. What's the worst outcome if I'm wrong? What's reversible, and what's permanent? And — this is the one people skip — what would I do if I weren't allowed to gather any more information?

Sometimes the right move is to decide now with what you have. Sometimes it's to buy time. But don't let "I need more data" become a way to avoid the discomfort of choosing.

I don't know the decision you're facing, so I can't tell you which it is. But I suspect that if you wrote one page explaining the choice to a stranger, you'd know more than you think.
